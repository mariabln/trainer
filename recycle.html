1. Introduction

Invented in 1859 by Gaston Planté. Oldest type of rechargeable battery still widely used.

  • Low cost
  • Robustness
  • High surge current capabilities

Key Applications:

  • Energy storage
  • Automotive
  • Backup power systems

Environmental Focus:

  • High recyclability (>95%)
  • Importance of clean recovery processes

2. Chemistry of Lead-Acid Batteries

2.1 Electrochemical Reactions

Discharge: PbO₂ + Pb + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O

Charge: 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → PbO₂ + Pb + 2H₂SO₄

2.2 Components

  • Positive Electrode: Lead dioxide (PbO₂)
  • Negative Electrode: Sponge lead (Pb)
  • Electrolyte: Dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)
  • Separator: Microporous material to prevent short circuits

3. Types of Lead-Acid Batteries

3.1 Flooded (Vented) Lead-Acid

Traditional "wet cell" design with liquid electrolyte. Requires maintenance, ventilation, and regular checks.

Subtypes:

  • SLI Batteries: High-current bursts, engine starting (cars, motorcycles, trucks)
  • Industrial Batteries:
    • Stationary: UPS, telecom, lighting
    • Traction: Forklifts, buses, pallet trucks

3.2 Valve-Regulated Lead-Acid (VRLA)

Sealed, maintenance-free, spill-proof with pressure relief valves. Safer and flexible in orientation.

  • AGM: Absorbed electrolyte, fast charging, low resistance
  • Gel Cell: Silica-gelled electrolyte, deep-cycle, sensitive to overcharging

3.3 Enhanced Types

  • Advanced AGM: Longer life, better charge acceptance
  • Carbon-Enhanced LABs: Better cycling, sulfation resistance

4. Structural Design and Construction

  • Plates: Lead alloy grids
  • Separators: Prevent short circuits
  • Container: Polypropylene
  • VRLA Valves: Pressure control
  • Use of recycled lead plates (if processed well)

5. Cycling Technology and Performance

5.1 Charge/Discharge Cycles

  • Cycle life: 500–1000
  • Charge efficiency: 70–85%
  • VRLA: Better for deep discharge

5.2 Sulfation

PbSO₄ crystals reduce capacity over time. Reversible early on.

Mitigation:

  • Equalization charging
  • Carbon additives

5.3 Cycling Enhancements

  • Additives: Barium sulfate, carbon
  • Refined secondary lead for performance

6. Applications

  • Automotive: SLI batteries, limited EV use
  • Energy Storage: Solar PV, UPS
  • Industrial: Forklifts, telecom towers

7. Recycling and Environmental Aspects

7.1 Standard Recycling Process

  • Breaking & separation
  • Desulfurization
  • Smelting & refining
  • Lead recovery

7.2 Emerging Technologies

  • Hydrometallurgy
  • Electrohydrometallurgy
  • Vacuum Reduction
  • Electrowinning
  • Alkaline Conversion

7.3 Environmental Risks

Informal recycling → soil & water pollution, especially in developing regions.

8. Policy, Sustainability, and Market Trends

8.1 Regulatory Efforts

  • China: LAB collection
  • EU: Battery Directive 2006/66/EC

8.2 Circular Economy

  • Reused for gamma-ray shielding, Zn-ion batteries

8.3 Market Forecasts

  • Global LAB demand rising
  • Secondary lead production increasing

9. Challenges and Future Prospects

  • Toxicity & emissions → cleaner processes
  • Performance limits: ~35 Wh/kg vs. Li-ion >150 Wh/kg
  • Innovation: Hybrid systems, smart BMS, green recycling

10. Conclusion

LABs remain critical for reliability-focused applications in developing regions, backup power, and sustainable reuse systems.
